# Mitochondrial coupling and red light

The electron transport chain is the engine of cellular ATP production. Four protein complexes, embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane, pass electrons from NADH and FADH2 down a redox gradient, pumping protons into the intermembrane space. ATP synthase then uses the proton motive force to phosphorylate ADP. The efficiency of this process — the ratio of ATP produced per oxygen consumed — is called mitochondrial coupling.

## Complex IV and the 670 nm window

Cytochrome c oxidase, also called Complex IV, is the terminal enzyme of the electron transport chain. It catalyzes the reduction of O2 to water, using four electrons donated by cytochrome c. Its copper-heme active site absorbs light across a broad spectrum, with a pronounced peak at 670 nm — deep red, bordering near-infrared. When Complex IV absorbs photons in this wavelength, it dissociates nitric oxide bound to the copper center, which had been acting as a competitive inhibitor against oxygen binding. The result is increased electron flux, increased proton pumping, and increased ATP output.

This is the mechanism behind photobiomodulation therapy, which exposes skin, joints, or specific organs to red or near-infrared light sources. Clinical trials show modest but reproducible effects on wound healing, thyroid function, retinal degeneration, and cognitive performance. The dose window matters: too little light produces no effect, too much downregulates the response. Typical protocols are 5-20 minutes of exposure at 20-100 mW/cm² surface power density.

## Uncoupling and brown adipose tissue

Uncoupling protein 1 (UCP1) dissipates the proton gradient as heat rather than ATP. It's highly expressed in brown adipose tissue (BAT), found in depots along the neck, shoulder blades, and around major vessels. Cold exposure activates BAT via sympathetic norepinephrine release, triggering lipolysis and UCP1-mediated thermogenesis. A single cold shower session can raise resting metabolic rate for 1-3 hours afterward, and repeated exposure increases BAT density over months.

Thyroid hormone is the other major driver of UCP1 expression. T3 binds nuclear receptors that upregulate UCP1 transcription. Hypothyroid individuals have reduced BAT thermogenesis and are more cold-intolerant; this isn't just psychological.

## Reactive oxygen species are signals

The textbook framing of mitochondrial ROS as damage is incomplete. Complex I and Complex III release superoxide during normal operation, and the cell uses this signal to regulate HIF-1α, NRF2, and sirtuin pathways. Aggressive antioxidant supplementation — vitamin C megadoses, NAC, alpha-lipoic acid all at once — can blunt these signals and paradoxically reduce mitochondrial adaptation to exercise. The sweet spot is matching the endogenous redox state rather than quenching it.

## Methylene blue

At low doses (1-2 mg/kg), methylene blue acts as an alternative electron donor at Complex IV, bypassing inhibited segments of the chain. It's used clinically for methemoglobinemia and has been investigated for Alzheimer's disease, septic shock, and post-COVID fatigue. Higher doses (above 4 mg/kg) shift the effect toward oxidation, and MB is also an MAO inhibitor — so combining with SSRIs, SNRIs, or tryptophan supplements risks serotonin syndrome.
