# Circadian rhythm and the light-dark signal

The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in the hypothalamus is the master clock. It receives direct retinal input via melanopsin-expressing intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs), which fire in response to blue light around 480 nm — a wavelength that matches daytime sky and is largely absent from sunrise, sunset, or firelight. This is how the body knows what time it is: not by the clock, but by the color temperature of the available light.

## Melatonin suppression by evening light

Melatonin production in the pineal gland begins roughly 14 hours after morning light onset, typically 9-10 PM for someone who wakes at 7 AM. Even modest evening light suppresses this release, delaying sleep onset. Studies measuring melatonin onset times find that 30 minutes of phone use at 100 lux equivalent can delay onset by 90 minutes or more. The suppressed melatonin isn't just about feeling sleepy — melatonin is also a potent antioxidant that orchestrates nightly tissue repair, and chronic suppression correlates with cancer risk, particularly breast and prostate.

Blue-blocking glasses worn after sunset restore melatonin onset in about 80% of users in lab studies. The cheap orange or red-tinted lenses are more effective than expensive "computer glasses" that only block a narrow blue band. Smartphones have night-shift modes that shift display color temperature toward red, but these still emit in the melanopsin-sensitive range and provide only partial protection.

## Peripheral clocks and food timing

The SCN isn't the only clock. Liver, muscle, gut, and adipose tissue each contain their own circadian machinery, synchronized primarily by food timing rather than light. Eating at biological night — say, a midnight snack after 4 hours of fasting — desynchronizes the liver clock from the SCN. This is why shift workers develop metabolic dysfunction even when sleep duration is preserved: the light signal and the food signal are pointing at different times.

Time-restricted eating (confining intake to a 6-10 hour window during daylight) realigns peripheral clocks with the SCN. Outcomes in controlled trials include improved insulin sensitivity, lower fasting glucose, and modest fat loss independent of total caloric intake. The time window matters more than the number of calories.

## Sleep pressure and adenosine

Wakefulness builds adenosine in the brain via ATP breakdown. Adenosine binding at A1 receptors in the basal forebrain is the homeostatic sleep pressure signal — this is what caffeine antagonizes, extending wakefulness by blocking adenosine rather than generating new energy. Caffeine's half-life is 5-8 hours; consumed at 3 PM, a quarter of the dose is still active at midnight, which is why late-day coffee shifts sleep onset even in people who don't "feel" its effects.

Sleep pressure and circadian drive for wakefulness interact multiplicatively. Sleep onset is easiest when pressure is high AND circadian drive is low — typically 10-11 PM for most chronotypes. A sleep opportunity at 7 PM, even with high adenosine after a long day, often fails because circadian alerting is still active. This is why jet lag affects sleep quality even more than sleep duration.
