# Grounding and the electrical environment

Grounding (also called earthing) is direct skin contact with conductive surfaces connected to the earth — bare feet on soil, damp grass, beach sand, or unsealed stone. The hypothesis is that the human body, typically at elevated positive electrical potential after hours indoors on insulating floors, equilibrates with the earth's surface charge when connected, and this equilibration has measurable physiological effects.

## The charge reservoir argument

The earth's surface carries a negative charge of roughly -300 V relative to the ionosphere, continuously replenished by global lightning activity. Soil conductivity allows this charge to spread and stabilize at ground level. When a human stands barefoot on conductive earth, body voltage drops from typical indoor values (often +1-10 V relative to ground) to near-zero within seconds. The redistributed electrons, the grounding hypothesis argues, act as antioxidants and attenuate inflammation.

The idea sounds fringe, but measurable effects have been reported. Published studies show reductions in blood viscosity measured by zeta potential, decreases in circulating inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6), and changes in autonomic nervous system balance toward parasympathetic dominance. Effect sizes are modest and vary widely between individuals; the literature is thin and largely from two or three research groups with financial interests in grounding products. Healthy skepticism is warranted.

## Wound healing and recovery

The most reproducible effect is in delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS) and exercise recovery. Randomized trials with sham controls have shown that grounding mats or patches during sleep after eccentric exercise reduce next-day soreness scores by 30-50% and shorten time to baseline by roughly a day. The proposed mechanism is reduced inflammatory response, but the acute effect happens too quickly for antioxidant pathways alone to explain it.

## Electromagnetic environment complications

The grounding hypothesis is complicated by the electromagnetic environment in modern homes. A grounded body can act as an antenna for AC mains-frequency (50/60 Hz) fields from house wiring, increasing rather than decreasing induced body voltage. Some "grounding" products that plug into the third prong of a wall outlet can amplify rather than reduce mains-frequency exposure, depending on wiring configuration. Truly passive earth contact — a wire buried in soil outside, connected via a rod — avoids this issue but is rare in commercial products.

## Practical protocols

For users interested in trying it without buying anything: 20-30 minutes of barefoot skin contact with damp grass, soil, beach sand, or bare stone is the baseline protocol. Concrete works if it's unsealed and in contact with earth. Asphalt, treated wood, tile, and vinyl are insulating. Studies have found the effect requires skin contact, not just being near the surface — shoes with rubber or synthetic soles block any transfer.

Morning grounding combines well with outdoor sunlight exposure: barefoot walking on dew-wet grass at sunrise hits circadian, UV hormone, and grounding signals in a single practice. Whether this is more than the sum of its parts is an open question.

## Safety

One clear contraindication: never ground outside during lightning storms. The "300 V earth potential" average becomes catastrophically irrelevant when a strike channels megavolts through the ground. Similarly, some cardiac pacemakers are sensitive to external electromagnetic interference; users with implanted devices should consult their cardiologist before adopting grounding protocols.
