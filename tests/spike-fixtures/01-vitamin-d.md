# Vitamin D is a hormone, not a vitamin

Vitamin D is misclassified. The name was coined in 1922 when Elmer McCollum identified a fat-soluble factor that prevented rickets, lettered D as the fourth micronutrient discovered after A, B, and C. A decade later, researchers realized the same compound could be synthesized endogenously in skin from 7-dehydrocholesterol when exposed to UVB light in the 290-315 nm range. Anything the body produces itself is, by definition, a hormone. Vitamin D is a secosteroid — structurally similar to cortisol, testosterone, and aldosterone — that signals through a nuclear receptor (VDR) expressed in nearly every tissue.

The photosynthesis reaction is entirely non-enzymatic. UVB breaks a carbon-carbon bond in 7-dehydrocholesterol, producing previtamin D3, which thermally isomerizes to cholecalciferol over a few hours at body temperature. No enzyme is involved in the skin step. This matters because factors that disable enzymes — age, toxins, genetic variants — don't affect the photonic reaction. Sunscreen at SPF 8 and above, however, blocks the reaction completely.

## The cholesterol sulfate hypothesis

Stephanie Seneff proposed that the active exported form from skin isn't cholecalciferol itself but cholesterol sulfate, a water-soluble variant produced alongside vitamin D during UV exposure. Cholesterol sulfate is known to be present in skin, circulates in high amounts in people with sun exposure, and is nearly absent in shielded populations. If true, oral D3 supplementation — which bypasses the skin step entirely — cannot replicate the circulating cholesterol sulfate fraction, and this may explain why supplemented populations with adequate serum 25-OH-D still show cardiovascular and metabolic outcomes worse than sun-exposed populations with equivalent serum levels.

## Latitude and seasonality

Above 37°N latitude, the solar zenith angle exceeds the threshold for UVB penetration from October through March. Boston, Madrid, Rome — all produce essentially zero vitamin D from sun during winter regardless of how much time is spent outdoors. Populations in these regions historically relied on dietary sources: fatty fish, liver, and fermented animal products provided a baseline winter supply. Modern diets have eliminated most of these sources, which is why winter deficiency is now the default rather than an exception.

## Cofactors matter more than dose

Supplementing D3 in isolation drives calcium mobilization without the corresponding signals that direct calcium to bone rather than soft tissue. Magnesium is required for the hydroxylation steps that convert cholecalciferol to its active form; deficiency here means oral D3 raises serum 25-OH-D but fails to produce the 1,25-(OH)2-D signal. Vitamin K2 (MK-7 or MK-4) directs calcium deposition via osteocalcin and matrix Gla protein; deficient K2 with high D3 increases arterial calcification risk.
